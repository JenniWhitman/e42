Computers are built on two states - high power states and lower power states also known as on and off or ones and zeros. These ones and zeros are meaningless without the math that supports them and math will not get very far with only two values... at least not very quickly. Thus, in order to use ones and zeros more efficiently, binary, or base 2 notation, can be used to express larger numbers from only the two. One digit in binary is often referred to as a bit. 8 of these make up a byte. Those are the building blocks of the memory within your computer. A little over one million of them store just one picture.

But how does it work? Base 2 uses the BASE number 2. Each digit in a number is the next power of 2. So, the first digit is 20 which equals 1. The next digit is 21 which is 2. Much later on, 2100 is 1267650600228229401496703205376. This carries on as long as it has to. The simplest way to think of it is that the numbers are then marked with a 1 when they are needed to make up the amount in question. This is base two.
